How to read the charts The charts show the percentage difference between the vote totals of both parties; for example, "R+5" means Republicans won the county by five percentage points. The shaded area on the charts marks +/- 5%; a line above the shaded area indicates Democratic strength, below the area indicates Republican strength. Hover over charts for details. Counties are arranged by 2018 population and list largest communities.

Denver

Denver's last dalliance with Republicans was when it went R+1 for Ronald Reagan in 1980. Since then it's been a steady march to Democratic stronghold.

El Paso

Colorado Springs • Colorado's second-largest county remains steadfastly Republican. It is trending more Republican again, as it was before margins narrowed in 2008.

Arapahoe

Aurora, Centennial • Denver's largest suburban county saw a huge swing in 2008, going strongly for Barack Obama and continuing that in 2016, going D+14.

Jefferson

Lakewood, Arvada • Like Arapahoe County, Jeffco went Democratic for the first time in 2008. It has remained in the D column, but with less enthusiasm.

Adams

Thornton, Westminster • Adams County went blue in 1988, 20 years before Arapahoe and Jeffco. It was D+9 in 2016 after having been double that in 2008.

Larimer

Fort Collins, Loveland • Since 1988, Larimer County has gone for the Republicans four times and for the Democrats four times, but has gone blue the last three elections.

Douglas

Highlands Ranch, Castle Rock • While still solidly Republican, Douglas County is not as red as it was before its Highlands Ranch-induced population boom in the 1990s.

Boulder

Boulder, Longmont • Boulder County has followed Denver's trajectory and is now solidly Democratic, though it first went blue in 1988, four years after Denver.

Weld

Greeley • Weld County has never been as deep red as El Paso or Douglas counties, but it's regaining its Republican footing after margins narrowed in 2008.

Pueblo

Pueblo County has been losing some of its Democratic strength since 1996, when its margin was D+23. It went slightly for Donald Trump in 2016, a 14-point swing from 2012.

Mesa

Grand Junction • Mesa County was R+7 in the Ross Perot-influenced 1992 election. Since then it has been a steady march to Republican domination, going R+36 in 2016.
